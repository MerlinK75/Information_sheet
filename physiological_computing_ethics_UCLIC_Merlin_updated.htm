1.      Invitation Paragraph

You are invited to take part in a research project. Before you decide whether to take part, it is important for you to understand why the research is being done and what participation will involve. Please take time to read the following information carefully and discuss it with others if you wish. Please ask us if there is anything that is not clear or if you would like more information. Take time to decide whether or not you wish to take part.

 

2.      What is the projects purpose?

The project aims to develop an understanding of users mindfulness states during meditation. Two artificial intelligent powered frameworks will be used to tailor feedback by reading your neural activity to determine what feedback creates a meditative state and for how long

 

3.      Why have I been chosen?

We are asking anyone who is at least 18 years of age, have little to no experience with meditation (novice meditator), and able to consent to volunteer for this study. This studies exclusion criteria consists of obstructions to administering the EEG to your head, such as wearing a wig, weave, braids, or any hair type that may be considered too dense for the EEG gel to pass through. Additionally, participants are requested to abstain from alcohol, caffeine, tobacco, and recreational drugs 4 hours before each session as these substances are known to affect EEG activity.

 

4.      Do I have to take part?

It is up to you to decide whether or not to take part. If you do decide to take part, you will be given this information sheet to keep and be asked to sign a consent form. You will also be reminded that you can withdraw from the study at any time during the duration of the experiment. You can also withdraw data I have provided, up to 2 days after the last data you submitted, without giving a reason. If you choose to withdraw, the data will no longer be used. You will not in any way be penalised for withdrawing from the study.

 

5.      What will happen to me if I take part?

You will be asked to use a sensor (EEG) that will capture and record your neural activity in an indoor, quiet space while meditating for periods that add up to approximately 2 hours. This will be broken up by 1 hour on two separate days which will be organised to suit your availability. You can take a break or stop participating in the study any time you need.

 

First, you will be asked to fill out surveys to allow the researcher to understand your meditation experience. We would additionally ask you for demographic information (gender, age, ethnicity, dominant hand), as some factors may affect the feedbacks effectiveness. You do not have to answer any questions you do not feel comfortable with answering. The researcher will need to help you put on the EEG which may require physical contact. You will be explained this in advance and ask if you feel comfortable. If you do not feel comfortable, you can decide to withdraw from the study without giving an explanation or be penalized. The EEG is administered on the head (like a hat) causing light pressure, and gel is applied which may appear cold for the user which both may cause discomfort for different individuals.

 

After equipping the EEG as stated above, you will be asked to meditate in a quiet room with noise cancelling headphones for a period of 10 minutes. If you are unsure how to meditate, guiding audio will discuss the proper techniques on focused attention meditation where you will be focusing on your breath. The meditation task will be followed with some time to rest and questions on your overall experience through surveys and open ended questions from the researcher. These questions may be recorded so they can be transcribed for analysis with the original recordings, and being deleted after transcribing. If you dont give consent to the audio recordings, the researcher will record on paper key points of your answers instead. Once rested, you will be asked to meditate again with audio feedback for another 10 minutes. Once completed, you can remove the headphones and EEG and you will be asked questions similar to the previous rest period, but this time on the feedback and how it was in comparison with no feedback. After this session has been complete, the researcher will book the next session for you on a date and time thats convenient. The next session will replicate this experiment, the only difference being a different audio feedback mechanism will be used which has the same goal of assisting with meditation but achieves this through a different methods/policy.

 

The EEG and headphones will be cleaned before you use them and will be cleaned after use as well.

7.      What are the possible benefits of taking part?

Whilst there are no immediate benefits for those people participating in the project, it is hoped that this work will assist general populations to start a meditation habit who struggle to start this habit without assistance.

 

8.      What if something goes wrong?

Extreme care will be taken in this research. However, if you wish to complain or have any concerns that are not addressed by the researcher, you should contact Dr. Youngjun Cho (youngjun.cho@ucl.ac.uk) who is the Principal Investigator on the project. If you further feel that your complaint has not been handled to your satisfaction, you can contact the Chair of the UCL Research Ethics Committee (ethics@ucl.ac.uk). If discomfort is experienced at any time during the experiment, you can take off the equipped devices and withdraw from the experiment if desired.

 

9.      Will my taking part in this project be kept confidential?

Your name and contact details will be kept strictly confidential. The audio captured will also be kept strictly confidential and only used as outlined in (6b) above..

 

10.   Limits to confidentiality

Confidentiality will be respected unless there are compelling and legitimate reasons for this to be breached. If this happens, we would inform you of any decisions that might limit your confidentiality.

 

11.   What will happen to the results of the research project?

The findings of our analysis of the data collected from the participants of the research project will be published in reports and articles and presented at public engagement and research talk venue. You will not be identifiable in these publications and presentations, except if you give us permission to include examples from the videos we collect from you. In any case, your name or contact details will never be included in publications and presentations.

 

The sensor data we collect from you and your self-reports, neither of which can be used to personally identify you, will be made open for use by other researchers for the benefit of further research; but only if you give us permission to do so.
